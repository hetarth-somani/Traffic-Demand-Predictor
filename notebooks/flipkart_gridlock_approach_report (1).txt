1. PROBLEM STATEMENT
════════════════════

Objective:
  Predict continuous traffic demand at 15-minute granularity across multiple
  geographic locations (geohash-encoded cells).

Evaluation Metric:
  score = max(0,  100 × R²(y_actual, y_predicted))
  — Computed on the original (non-transformed) demand scale.
  — R² below 0 is clipped to 0; perfect prediction → 100.

Train Size : 77,299 rows, 11 columns (includes target `demand`)
Test Size  : 41,778 rows, 10 columns (no target)

The challenge is fundamentally a time-series forecasting problem where:
  • Locations are encoded as geohash strings (high cardinality, ~400+ cells).
  • The data covers an extremely short window (~26 hours total).
  • Test set requires predictions 2–13.75 hours ahead of the last training point.





2. DATASET DESCRIPTION & EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS (EDA)
══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

2.1 Raw Column Overview
────────────────────────
  Column              Type         Notes
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  geohash6            string       Location identifier (~400+ unique cells)
  day                 integer      Day index; train = {48, 49}, test ⊂ {49}
  timestamp           string       Time-of-day only (format: "H:MM", e.g. "0:15")
  Temperature         float        Ambient temperature reading
  NumberofLanes       integer      Road capacity proxy
  [road_type cols]    categorical  One-hot / label-encoded road attributes
  [weather cols]      categorical  Weather condition indicators
  demand              float        TARGET — 15-min traffic demand volume

2.2 Critical Time Structure Discovery
───────────────────────────────────────
  The single most important EDA finding:

    • `timestamp` is NOT a full datetime — it is only a time-of-day string
      (e.g., "6:30" means 6:30 AM on whatever day is in the `day` column).

    • Data spans approximately 26 hours:
        Train : day 48 (full 24 h, 96 slots) + first 2 h of day 49 (8 slots)
        Test  : day 49 from 02:15 to 13:45  → 47 × 15-min slots

    • True timestamp was reconstructed as:
        _ts = base_datetime + (day - 48) × 24h + parsed_time_of_day
        slot = (day - 48) × 96 + (hour × 60 + minute) / 15

    • This reconstruction was essential for correct lag semantics.
      Without it, "lag-96" (one day back) was computed incorrectly.

2.3 Demand Distribution
────────────────────────
  • Demand is right-skewed with high-demand spikes at certain geohash cells.
  • Clear 15-minute periodicity within each day (morning ramp, mid-day plateau,
    evening peak, overnight trough).
  • Day 49 morning (02:15–13:45) captures the ramp-up from overnight low to
    near-peak hours — the hardest window to predict iteratively.

2.4 Geohash Analysis
─────────────────────
  • ~400+ unique geohash cells of varying demand levels.
  • High-volume geohashes (highways, junctions) exhibit sharp morning spikes.
  • Low-volume geohashes remain nearly flat — easy to predict, contribute little
    to the overall R² signal.
  • Target encoding per geohash (fold-aware) was the single most impactful
    feature beyond lags.

2.5 Temporal Patterns
──────────────────────
  • Demand auto-correlation is strongest at lag-1 (15 min back) and lag-4 (1 h).
  • Day-over-day patterns (lag-96) are meaningful but we have only one full day
    of prior data, so lag-96 == lag across the single day boundary.
  • Rolling statistics over 3–6 slots capture short bursts; over 12–24 slots
    capture trend level.





3. FEATURE ENGINEERING
═══════════════════════

All lag and rolling features were computed STRICTLY within each training fold
to prevent data leakage. Validation and test predictions were generated using
an iterative one-step-ahead loop (see §4.2).

3.1 Temporal Features
──────────────────────
  Feature                 Description
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  hour                    Integer hour of day (0–23)
  minute                  Minute within hour (0, 15, 30, 45)
  slot_of_day             Slot index 0–95 within a 24-h day
  slot_global             Global slot index across all days
  hour_sin / hour_cos     Sine/cosine encoding of hour (captures cyclicality)
  slot_sin / slot_cos     Sine/cosine encoding of slot_of_day
  is_peak_morning         Binary flag: slot in typical morning peak range
  is_overnight            Binary flag: slot in overnight low-demand range

3.2 Lag Features (per geohash)
───────────────────────────────
  lag_1                   Demand 15 min ago (same geohash)
  lag_2                   Demand 30 min ago
  lag_4                   Demand 1 hour ago
  lag_8                   Demand 2 hours ago
  lag_12                  Demand 3 hours ago
  lag_24                  Demand 6 hours ago
  lag_48                  Demand 12 hours ago
  lag_96                  Demand 24 hours ago (one full day back)

  ⚠ LEAKAGE CONTROL: Lags are always built from past rows only within each
  fold's training window. The iterative inference loop (§4.2) feeds model
  predictions back as lag inputs for subsequent slots.

3.3 Rolling Statistics (per geohash)
──────────────────────────────────────
  roll_mean_3             Rolling mean over last 3 slots (45 min)
  roll_mean_6             Rolling mean over last 6 slots (90 min)
  roll_mean_12            Rolling mean over last 12 slots (3 hours)
  roll_std_3              Rolling std over last 3 slots (volatility)
  roll_std_6              Rolling std over last 6 slots
  roll_max_12             Rolling max over last 12 slots
  roll_min_12             Rolling min over last 12 slots
  roll_trend_6            Linear trend slope over last 6 slots (rise/fall rate)

3.4 Geohash Encoding
─────────────────────
  geohash_target_enc      Smoothed mean-target encoding of geohash, re-fitted
                          on training portion of each fold to prevent leakage.
                          Smoothing factor = 10 (count-weighted blend with
                          global mean).

  geohash_std_enc         Smoothed std-of-target encoding (demand volatility
                          per location — helps model distinguish stable vs.
                          volatile cells).

3.5 Static Features (passed as-is)
────────────────────────────────────
  Temperature, NumberofLanes, and all road/weather categorical columns.
  Categoricals were label-encoded once globally (consistent across folds).

3.6 Calibration Feature
────────────────────────
  After iterative prediction on the validation set, a median ratio was computed:
    calibration_factor = median(y_val_actual) / median(y_val_predicted)
  This scalar factor was multiplied into all test predictions (+0.05 LB gain).





4. MODELLING ARCHITECTURE
══════════════════════════

4.1 Primary Model: LightGBM (Gradient Boosted Trees)
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  We chose LightGBM as our primary learner for the following reasons:
    • Handles high-cardinality categoricals efficiently.
    • Fast training — critical for iterative fold-aware pipelines.
    • Strong performance on tabular time-series with lag features.
    • Native support for sample weights (used for recency weighting).

  Final Hyperparameters (post-Optuna tuning):
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────
    num_leaves         : 127
    learning_rate      : 0.03
    n_estimators       : 1200
    min_child_samples  : 20
    subsample          : 0.85
    colsample_bytree   : 0.80
    reg_alpha          : 0.1
    reg_lambda         : 1.0
    objective          : regression
    metric             : rmse
    verbose            : -1

4.2 Iterative One-Step-Ahead Forecasting
─────────────────────────────────────────
  This was the CORE innovation that fixed the CV–LB gap:

  Standard approach (broken):
    Compute all lag features upfront using the full dataset.
    → Lag features in the test set reference future values that don't exist.
    → Constant proxies (last training value) become stale over long horizons.

  Our iterative approach (correct):
    for each time slot t in {val/test slots} in chronological order:
      1. Build row for slot t using only predictions from slots < t.
      2. Predict demand at slot t → ŷ_t
      3. Insert ŷ_t into the lag buffer.
      4. Next slot's lag_1 = ŷ_t, lag_2 = ŷ_{t-1}, etc.

  This exactly mirrors how predictions would be used in real deployment and
  ensures that the validation score correlates with the leaderboard score.

4.3 Bagging Ensemble (10-Seed)
───────────────────────────────
  To reduce variance in the iterative loop:
    • Trained 10 identical LightGBM models with different random seeds.
    • During inference, ran the iterative loop independently for each seed.
    • Final prediction = mean of all 10 iterative prediction sequences.
  
  Rationale: Each seed produces slightly different lag-propagation trajectories;
  averaging smooths out prediction instability without changing model structure.

4.4 Hyperparameter Tuning: Optuna
───────────────────────────────────
  Framework : Optuna (TPE sampler, 100 trials)
  CV setup  : 3-fold expanding-window on day 48 only
              (each fold: train N slots → iteratively predict next 16 slots)
  Objective : Maximise mean iterative R² across 3 folds

  Key finding: Optuna-tuned parameters improved the 2-hour day-split CV
  from 91.68 → 91.84 but did NOT push the LB past 89.9, confirming that
  short-horizon tuning does not generalise to the full 47-slot test horizon.



5. VALIDATION STRATEGY
═══════════════════════

5.1 Day-Split Validation (Primary)
────────────────────────────────────
  Train  : All of day 48 (96 slots, 77,299 rows minus day 49 rows)
  Validate: Day 49 first 2 hours (8 slots, ~3,200 rows)
  Method : Iterative one-step-ahead inference on the 8 validation slots.
  Score  : 91.84 R²

  Why: Most realistic split available. Directly mirrors the train→test boundary.
  Limitation: Only 8 steps — significantly shorter than the 47-step test horizon.

5.2 Slot-Based 5-Fold CV (Secondary)
──────────────────────────────────────
  Folds defined by time slot boundaries (not row index).
  Each fold validates ~20 consecutive slots using iterative inference.
  Mean CV : 80.52 ± 15.64

  High variance arises because some folds validate over demand cliff edges
  (e.g., late-night drop-off) while the test is a morning ramp-up only.

5.3 Long-Horizon CV (Proposed Final Fix)
─────────────────────────────────────────
  Train on first 60 slots of day 48 → iteratively predict last 36 slots.
  36 slots ≈ 9 hours, closer to the 11.5-hour test horizon.
  This score correlates better with LB and should guide all future decisions.

5.4 Anti-Leakage Measures
──────────────────────────
  ✓ Lag features rebuilt inside every fold training window.
  ✓ Target encoding re-fitted per fold on training portion only.
  ✓ Validation/test lags populated iteratively from model predictions.
  ✓ Calibration factor derived from validation fold, not from test.
  ✗ Global lag precomputation (tried, rejected — inflated CV by ~5 points).

7. WHAT WE TRIED & REJECTED
═════════════════════════════

  Approach                        Why Rejected / What Failed
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Global lag precomputation       Leaked future data into CV; R² inflated by
  before cross-validation         ~5 pts; CV-LB gap collapsed model trust.

  Row-based TimeSeriesSplit       Ignored uneven sample density; one fold had
                                  a 7-hour gap → R² of 0.53 in that fold.
                                  Replaced by slot-based splits.

  No-lag model (static features   Without lags, model cannot learn demand
  only)                           dynamics. R² dropped to ~82. Lags are
                                  non-negotiable with only 1 day of history.

  Constant lag proxies for test   Last-training-value proxies go stale over
  (non-iterative)                 the 11.5-hour test horizon. Fixed by full
                                  iterative inference loop.

  LightGBM + CatBoost stacking    CatBoost underperformed LightGBM alone.
  with meta-learner               OOF set too small → meta-model overfit.
                                  Net LB impact was negative.

  Trend-adjusted proxy methods    Hand-crafted trend increments introduced
                                  arbitrary assumptions; iterative loop is
                                  strictly more principled.

  XGBoost as drop-in replacement  Requires full re-engineering of iterative
                                  loop. Not trialled due to time constraints.

  LSTM / sequence models          Only 96 time steps per geohash (1 day).
                                  Too few samples for LSTM generalisation;
                                  risk of overfitting the single day pattern.

  Log-transform of demand         Minimal R² improvement; adds complexity in
                                  back-transformation and calibration.

  Winsorizing outliers            Clipping high-demand geohash spikes reduced
                                  model's ability to capture peak hours.

  Optuna tuning on leaky CV       Best parameters from leaky pipeline were
                                  meaningless; tuning restarted from scratch
                                  after fixing the CV pipeline.

8. RESULTS & LEADERBOARD PERFORMANCE
═══════════════════════════════════════

  Experiment                               Day-Split CV    Public LB
  ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Baseline LightGBM (no lags, leaky CV)    ~88.0           ~85.0
  + Lags + Optuna (leaky CV)               ~90.5           ~86.0
  Model C: Iterative + fold-aware lags     91.68           89.9
  + Median-ratio calibration               91.68           ~90.0 (est.)
  + Optuna on iterative CV                 91.84           89.9
  + 10-seed bagging                        91.84           89.9  ← BEST

  Key Gap Analysis:
  • Day-split CV (8 steps):  91.84  → short-horizon, optimistic
  • Slot-based CV (20 steps): 80.52 → high variance, not reliable
  • Public LB (47 steps):    89.9   → true long-horizon performance
  • Iterative CV day48 (16 slots): ~79.5 → closer proxy for LB behaviour

  The gap between day-split (91.84) and LB (89.9) is caused by error
  accumulation in the iterative loop over 47 steps. The model was implicitly
  optimised for 8-step horizons; 47-step performance degrades due to
  compounding lag-prediction errors.




9. LESSONS LEARNED & FUTURE DIRECTIONS
═══════════════════════════════════════

9.1 Core Lessons
─────────────────
  1. Fix evaluation first, tune second.
     The moment our CV pipeline was leaky, every tuning decision was
     meaningless. Three days of Optuna tuning on a leaky setup taught us
     nothing. The honest iterative CV was the foundation for everything.

  2. Validation horizon must match test horizon.
     A 2-hour validation window does not predict behaviour on a 11.5-hour
     test window. Always create at least one validation fold whose length
     equals the test length.

  3. Iterative inference is mandatory for multi-step lag models.
     Any model that uses its own past predictions as future lag inputs must
     be evaluated iteratively, not with constant proxies.

  4. Data volume constraints are real.
     With only 26 hours of data, complex architectures (LSTM, stacking)
     are high-risk. Simple, interpretable models with strong lag features
     win on tiny temporal datasets.

9.2 Future Directions (to push past 90.0)
───────────────────────────────────────────
  Priority 1 — Long-horizon validation (36-slot CV on day48)
    Use this as the primary metric for all future decisions. It correlates
    far better with the LB than the current 8-slot day-split.

  Priority 2 — Error accumulation analysis
    Simulate the iterative loop on day48 and plot R² vs forecast horizon
    (1-step, 2-step, …, 36-step). Identify where the model systematically
    under- or over-predicts and design a correction.

  Priority 3 — Sample weighting (recency bias)
    Weight training rows by exponential decay (more recent rows weighted
    higher). This biases the model toward accuracy at the start of the
    test period, reducing initial lag errors that compound downstream.

  Priority 4 — Trend-aware lag buffer inside iterative loop
    Estimate the average demand rise rate per geohash during morning hours
    from training data. Add a small dynamic increment to lag values as the
    loop progresses to prevent lag features from systematically lagging
    behind a rising demand trend.

  Priority 5 — XGBoost parallel ensemble
    Train XGBoost with the same iterative pipeline. Take a fixed weighted
    average (no meta-learner) of LightGBM + XGBoost predictions.
